2The big picture ↑ top

Both topics use the same kind of data: two scores per person (X and Y), called bivariate data. The difference is what you do with it.

Topic 4: CorrelationTopic 5: Regression
Describes the relationship: how strongly, and in what direction, X and Y move together. The output is r.Uses the relationship to predict one variable from the other. The output is a line, Y′ = a + b(X).
Same engine underneath: means → deviations → SS → SD → SP → COV → r. Regression just keeps going to build a prediction line.

3Foundations ↑ top

  • Univariate is one variable (describe it with a graph plus mean and SD). Bivariate is two variables, X and Y.
  • For bivariate data you graph it with a scatterplot, describe each variable (mean, SD), then describe the association with r.
  • Correlation is a technique that measures and describes the relationship between two variables. You need two scores per individual, usually measured rather than manipulated. Knowing two things are correlated lets you make predictions.

4Describing a correlation ↑ top

A correlation is described four ways. The first three make up your "describe it in words" answer.

WayMeansValues
1. DirectionWhich way they move togetherPositive (+): X↑ Y↑. Negative (−): X↑ Y↓. None (0).
2. ShapeStraight line or curveLinear, or curvilinear (e.g. an inverted U)
3. StrengthHow tightly points cluster (subjective)Weak, moderate, strong, none
4. MagnitudeThe computed number (objective)r, from −1 to +1
High-yield When asked to describe a relationship, give three terms: strength, direction, shape, like "strong, negative, linear." That's exactly what the homework key wanted.

What the patterns look like

Strong positive
Strong negative
No relationship (≈0)
Curvilinear (inverted U)
Think of it like Strength is how reliably a shooter's form predicts the make. A player who drains it from the same spot every time gives you a tight, strong relationship (points hug the line). A streaky chucker is weak (points scattered). Direction is just which way it leans: more arc means more makes, so that's positive.

Interpreting r: magnitude thresholds

For human behaviour, |r|Interpretation
< .10No relationship
.10 to .30Weak
> .30 to .50Moderate
> .50Strong
For test reliability, |r| (higher bar)Interpretation
> .85Very desirable
.70 to .85Desirable / moderately acceptable
< .70Poor reliability
Trap The two tables are different. A reliability question (test-retest, inter-rater, internal consistency) needs a much higher r than an ordinary human-behaviour relationship. Don't call r = .65 "strong" on a reliability question; there, it's poor reliability.

5Pearson r: what & why ↑ top

Pearson r (also called the Pearson product-moment correlation) measures the degree and direction of the linear relationship between two variables. Both variables must be interval/ratio. The population symbol is ρ (rho); the sample is r. It runs from −1 to +1: closer to ±1 is stronger, closer to 0 is weaker.

The logic is the Venn-diagram idea. Each variable has its own variance, and where they overlap is the shared variance, or covariance.

r = (how much X and Y vary TOGETHER) / (how much X and Y vary SEPARATELY) = covariability of X and Y / total variability of X and Y

Perfect overlap gives r = ±1. No overlap gives r = 0.

6Calculating r ↑ top

This is the spine of the whole calculation question. Learn the order.

1. Plot the data (scatterplot) 2. For EACH variable: mean (x̄ = Σx/N), then SS = Σ(x−x̄)², then SD = √(SS/N) 3. Sums of Products: SP = Σ(x−x̄)(y−ȳ) 4. Covariance: COV = SP / N 5. Correlation: r = COV / (SD_x · SD_y)
Worked example: meditation vs concentration (from your slides)

X = minutes meditating per week: 20, 0, 10, 12, 3. Y = minutes concentrating: 30, 5, 21, 29, 10. (N = 5)

Means. x̄ = 45/5 = 9. ȳ = 95/5 = 19.
Deviations & SS (X). (x−x̄) = 11, −9, 1, 3, −6. Squared: 121, 81, 1, 9, 36. SSx = 248. SDx = √(248/5) = √49.6 = 7.0427.
Deviations & SS (Y). (y−ȳ) = 11, −14, 2, 10, −9. Squared: 121, 196, 4, 100, 81. SSy = 502. SDy = √(502/5) = √100.4 = 10.0200.
SP. Products (x−x̄)(y−ȳ) = 121, 126, 2, 30, 54. SP = 333.
COV. 333/5 = 66.6.
r. 66.6 / (7.0427 × 10.0200) = .94. Strong, positive, linear.
Report (APA style). "There was a strong positive relationship between minutes meditating (M = 9.00, SD = 7.04) and minutes concentrating (M = 19.00, SD = 10.02), r = .94."
Tidy trick Build one table with columns: X, Y, (x−x̄), (y−ȳ), (x−x̄)², (y−ȳ)², (x−x̄)(y−ȳ). Sum the last three columns to get SSx, SSy, and SP. Everything else flows from there, which is exactly how the answer keys lay it out.

7Factors that affect r ↑ top

First, the one that does NOT affect r Sample size. "Bigger samples give bigger r" is false, because N cancels out in the formula. Don't confuse this with sampling error, which is a different thing and can affect r.

Seven factors that do affect the size of r:

FactorWhat it does
1. Real relationshipThe relationship genuinely is strong or weak.
2. Sampling errorThe luck-of-the-draw gap between your sample and the true population. It can push r spuriously up or down.
3. Unmeasured third variableA hidden variable drives both. This is why correlation isn't causation; you'd need an experiment. Example: ice cream sales and drownings (both driven by summer heat).
4. Heterogeneous sampleYour sample is really two subgroups. Combining them can fake a relationship (kids plus adults for height and weight) or hide opposite patterns within each group.
5. Restricted (truncated) rangeYou sampled only part of the range, so r changes and you can't generalize beyond what you sampled. It can raise r (cutting off a curve) or lower it (chopping a linear trend into a blob).
6. Non-linearity (curvilinear)If the true relationship is a curve, Pearson r underestimates it. Plot first.
7. HeteroscedasticityThe spread of Y changes across X (a fan shape; bad), usually from a skewed variable. Since r = COV/(SDxSDy), a bigger SD means lower r. (Homoscedasticity is constant spread, which is good.)

8Which correlation to use ↑ top

Pearson needs both variables interval/ratio. For other scales you use the same formula under a different name. Pick using the scale of measurement of each variable.

Y: Nominal (2 groups)Y: OrdinalY: Interval/Ratio
X: Nominal (2 groups)Phi (φ)Spearman (rs)Point-Biserial (rpb)
X: OrdinalSpearmanSpearmanSpearman
X: Interval/RatioPoint-Biserial (rpb)SpearmanPearson (r)
Decision rule (say it in words for full marks)
  • Both interval/ratio: Pearson r
  • Any variable is ordinal: Spearman rho (rs)
  • One dichotomous nominal (2 groups) plus one interval/ratio: Point-Biserial (rpb)
  • Both dichotomous nominal: Phi (φ)

Spearman rho also rescues two extra cases on interval/ratio data: a weak curvilinear relationship, or heteroscedasticity. It converts scores to ranks (then runs Pearson on the ranks), which measures consistency rather than exact form and pulls outliers in. So curvilinear or skewed/fan-shaped data points to Spearman.

x̄ = 30/5 = 6. (x−x̄) = −4, −2, 0, 2, 4. SSx = 16+4+0+4+16 = 40. SDx = √(40/5) = √8 = 2.83.
ȳ = 40/5 = 8. (y−ȳ) = −5, −1, 0, 2, 4. SSy = 25+1+0+4+16 = 46. SDy = √(46/5) = √9.2 = 3.03.
SP = (−4)(−5)+(−2)(−1)+0+(2)(2)+(4)(4) = 20+2+0+4+16 = 42. COV = 42/5 = 8.40.
r = 8.40 / (2.83 × 3.03) = .98. Strong, positive, linear.

9What regression adds ↑ top

Correlation tells you the relationship exists. Regression uses it to predict an unknown score from a known one.

  • Predictor variable: the one you predict from (X-axis).
  • Criterion variable: the one being predicted (Y-axis).
Think of it like Correlation says "hours practiced and free throws made go together." Regression builds the formula that lets you say a kid who practices 7 hours will probably make about 9 free throws. It's like a game predicting your rank from hours played: draw the line of best fit through the data, then read off the prediction.

10The equation & least squares ↑ top

Y′ = a_y + b_y(x) Y′ = "Y-prime" = the PREDICTED score (also written Ŷ) a_y = Y-intercept, b_y = slope

Error (residual) is a person's actual Y minus their predicted Y′, that is (Y − Y′). The best-fitting line minimizes that error.

Why "least squares" You can't just add up the errors Σ(Y−Y′), because they always sum to 0 (the line runs through the middle). So you square them and minimize Σ(Y−Y′)². The best-fitting line is the one where Σ(Y−Y′)² is as small as possible, which is why it's called least squares regression.

11Slope & intercept ↑ top

What it meansFormula
Slope byChange in Y for every 1-unit increase in X (rise over run). Can be + or −.by = r · (SDy / SDx)
Intercept ayValue of Y′ when X = 0 (where the line crosses the Y-axis).ay = ȳ − by(x̄)
Interpreting slope (a classic conceptual question) If Y′ = .15 + .27(X) predicts grad-school GPA (Y) from GRE score (X), the slope means GPA goes up by .27 for every 1-point increase in GRE. Slope is the change in the criterion per 1-unit rise in the predictor.
They can run it backwards Given any 3 of {r, by, SDx, SDy}, solve for the 4th from by = r·SDy/SDx. Example: r = .31, by = .42, SDy = 3.44, so SDx = r·SDy/by = .31×3.44/.42 = 2.54.

12Build & plot the line ↑ top

To define Y′ (predict Y from X): 1. Scatterplot + mean & SD for each variable 2. Pearson r 3. Slope: b_y = r · SD_y / SD_x 4. Intercept: a_y = ȳ − b_y(x̄) 5. Equation: Y′ = a_y + b_y(x) → plug in a given X to predict 6. Plot the line through TWO points: the intercept (0, a_y) and the means (x̄, ȳ) To define X′ (predict X from Y): same steps, swap X & Y → b_x = r · SD_x / SD_y a_x = x̄ − b_x(ȳ) X′ = a_x + b_x(y)
Worked example: your Homework 5 (ATRISK predicting IMOBULBN)

X = ATRISK score: 7, 7, 7, 2, 4, 4, 3, 8, 3, 5. Y = IMOBULBN: 25, 25, 28, 12, 21, 25, 15, 22, 9, 18. (N = 10)

Means & SDs. x̄ = 5, SSx = 40, so SDx = √(40/10) = 2.00. ȳ = 20, SSy = 358, so SDy = √(358/10) = 5.9833.
SP, COV, r. SP = 92, COV = 9.2, r = 9.2/(2×5.9833) = .77 (strong positive).
Slope. by = r·SDy/SDx = .7688 × 5.9833/2 = 2.30.
Intercept. ay = ȳ − by(x̄) = 20 − 2.30(5) = 8.50.
Equation. Y′ = 8.50 + 2.30(X).
Predict. X = 5 gives Y′ = 8.50 + 2.30(5) = 20.00. X = 10 gives NA, cannot extrapolate (10 is outside the observed X range of 2 to 8).
Plot it. Draw through the intercept (0, 8.50) and the means (5, 20). The X′ line crosses it at (5, 20).

13Predictions & extrapolation ↑ top

Easy points, don't miss it Regression does not predict beyond the range of the observed data. If the X you're asked to plug in is outside the X values you actually measured, you cannot predict; write "NA, cannot extrapolate." In HW5, X = 10 and Y = 3 were both out of range, so both were NA. This is a classic trap.

14Y′ vs X′: the two lines ↑ top

  • You can draw two regression lines: Y′ (predict Y from X) and X′ (predict X from Y).
  • They always cross at the means (x̄, ȳ). If yours don't, you made an error.
  • With raw scores, the two lines rotate around (x̄, ȳ) as r changes.
  • A small angle between them means a high correlation. At r = ±1 the lines overlap; as r approaches 0 the angle grows, and at r = 0 they're perpendicular (90°).

15How r, b, and a relate ↑ top

  • If r = 0, the slope b = 0. The Y′ line is flat at ay = ȳ (your best guess for everyone is just the mean of Y), and the X′ line is vertical at ax = x̄.
  • As r increases, |b| increases (a steeper line).
  • If b is positive, the intercept a sits below the mean, toward the origin. If b is negative, a sits above the mean, away from the origin.

16Standard error of estimate ↑ top

Standard error of estimate (SDY−Y′) is the standard deviation of the prediction errors (the residuals). It's how far off, on average, your predictions are. Same logic as a regular SD, but on (Y − Y′) instead of (x − x̄).
SD_(Y−Y′) = √( Σ(Y−Y′)² / N ) ← the "long way" SD_(Y−Y′) = SD_y · √(1 − r²) ← the fast way (use this)
If…then SDY−Y′ is…why
r ≠ 0smaller than SDyX gives extra info, so less prediction error (two sources beat one)
r = 0equal to SDyX tells you nothing; SDy·√(1−0) = SDy
r = ±10every point is on the line, so no error; SDy·√(1−1) = 0

17Variability: total, explained, unexplained ↑ top

This is the prof's favourite conceptual area. There are three kinds of variability, each measured from a data point.

TypeDistanceMeaning
Total(Y − ȳ)How far the actual score is from the mean of Y.
Explained (regression)(Y′ − ȳ)How far the prediction is from the mean: the part the X–Y relationship accounts for.
Unexplained (error/residual)(Y − Y′)How far the actual score is from the prediction: the leftover the line can't explain.
Total = Explained + Unexplained (Y − ȳ) = (Y′ − ȳ) + (Y − Y′) Squared (because raw deviations sum to 0): SS_T = SS_R + SS_E Σ(Y−ȳ)² = Σ(Y′−ȳ)² + Σ(Y−Y′)²

SS values are in awkward squared units, so convert them to proportions.

The punchline: r²
  • Proportion explained = SSR/SST = r²: the share of Y you can predict from X.
  • Proportion unexplained = SSE/SST = 1 − r²: the leftover, due to other unknown factors.
Example: r = .95 gives r² = .90, so 90% of the variability in Y is explained by X and 10% is unexplained.
Think of it like r² is the percent of a teammate's scoring you can explain by their minutes played. r² = .90 means minutes explain 90% of the point differences; the other 10% is hot and cold nights, defense, and luck, which is outside your one predictor.
✎ Practice: Part 2 (regression + full calculations)
C11. In Y′ = a + b(X), which is the predictor and which is the criterion?
X is the predictor (you predict from it); Y is the criterion (being predicted).
C12. Why does the best-fit line minimize the squared errors instead of the plain errors?
The plain errors Σ(Y−Y′) always sum to 0, since positives and negatives cancel. Squaring removes the cancelation, and minimizing Σ(Y−Y′)² gives the unique "least squares" line.
C13. A regression is Y′ = 12 + 3(X). Interpret the slope and intercept in plain words.
Slope: Y goes up by 3 for every 1-unit increase in X. Intercept: when X = 0, the predicted Y is 12.
C14. If r = 0, what do the two regression lines look like, and what's your best prediction of Y for anyone?
The lines are perpendicular (Y′ flat at ȳ, X′ vertical at x̄). With r = 0, your best prediction of Y for anyone is just ȳ, the mean; X adds nothing.
C15. r = .60. What proportion of the variance in Y is explained? Unexplained?
Explained = r² = .36 (36%). Unexplained = 1 − r² = .64 (64%).

18Attacking the Part 2 question ↑ top

When you open the calculation question, run this order every time. Build the big table first, and everything falls out of it.

  1. Draw the scatterplot (X on the horizontal axis, as instructed).
  2. Make the table: X, Y, (x−x̄), (y−ȳ), (x−x̄)², (y−ȳ)², (x−x̄)(y−ȳ).
  3. Column sums give SSx, SSy, SP. Then SDx, SDy, COV, r.
  4. Describe and interpret r (direction, strength, magnitude).
  5. by, then ay, then write Y′ = ay + by(X). Add bx, ax, X′ if asked.
  6. Predict for the given values, checking that each one is inside the data range (otherwise NA).
  7. If asked: SDY−Y′ = SDy√(1−r²), then r² and 1−r², then SST = SSR + SSE.
  8. Draw the regression line(s) through the intercept (0, a) and the means (x̄, ȳ). They must cross at the means.
For every calculation formula in notation, then numbers plugged in, then final answer (2 decimals). Write "NA" where a value shouldn't be computed. The part marks live in the work you show.
